Keeping out the enemy

Ireland was a dangerous place in the 11th century.

There was constant threats from neighbouring chieftains and Normans and even wild animals!

Defending the household was key in castle design in those days.

Outer walls

The walls surrounding the castle, called a bawn, were to protect the people and farm animals within and keep unwelcome visitors out.

Narrow windows

Narrow windows- not so good for flooding the room with light, but very handy if you need to shoot arrows at your enemy within being wounded in return.

Machicolations

From these holes at the roof of the castle, the defenders threw rocks, boiling water, boiling oil or tar at the enemy below.

Murder hole

If the enemy survived the walls, arrows, rocks and hot liquids from above, a defender, hidden in a hole above the front door was waiting with more. It's not called a murder hole for nothing!
